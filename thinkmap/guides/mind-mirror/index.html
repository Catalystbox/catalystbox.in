How to use Mind Mirror

Mind Mirror is the quiet one in the ThinkMap set. It doesn't price a decision — it holds up a mirror. You answer a series of honest statements about how you think, and it shows you the pattern back: how you see your own mind across eighteen kinds of thinking, and — the part most tools skip — how well you actually seem to know yourself. It is not an IQ test, and that's the point.

The one honest idea behind Mind Mirror

It measures your self-perception — not your ability. And that's exactly why it's useful.

What it is, and what it isn't

This distinction matters more for Mind Mirror than for any other tool in the set, so it's worth being blunt about it.

✦ It is

  • A structured way to reflect on how you see your own thinking
  • A snapshot of your self-image on the day you take it
  • A conversation starter — with yourself, or with people who know you
  • A gentle check on how well you know your own mind

It isn't

  • An IQ test or a measure of actual ability
  • A psychological assessment or a diagnosis
  • A verdict on your intelligence, potential, or worth
  • Something to decide anything real about a person

If you need an objective read on a specific ability, that calls for a validated instrument administered by a qualified professional. Mind Mirror is for reflection — nothing more, and nothing less.

The scale — what SD, D, N, A, SA mean

Every statement is answered on a five-point agreement scale. Each button shows a number and a short code; here's the full meaning of each:

1SD Strongly DisagreeNot like me at all — this rarely or never describes how I think or act.
2D DisagreeMostly not like me — this describes me only occasionally.
3N NeutralIn between, unsure, or it depends on the situation.
4A AgreeMostly like me — this describes me fairly often.
5SA Strongly AgreeVery much like me — this consistently describes how I think or act.

Answer for how you actually are, not how you'd like to be — the mirror is only as honest as you are. One statement is reverse-worded on purpose (about underrating yourself), so read each one carefully rather than pattern-tapping.

The five clusters, eighteen facets

The eighteen facets group into five clusters, so your results are readable at a glance and in depth if you want it.

Analytical & FluidLogical reasoning, spatial thinking, working memory, processing speed.
Knowledge & CommunicationVerbal ability, long-term recall, digital and technological fluency.
Social & EmotionalReading people, self-awareness, perceiving emotion in others.
Creative, Strategic & ReflectiveDivergent ideas, strategic foresight, meaning-making, and humility.
Applied & SensoryPractical execution, physical coordination, naturalistic and musical sense.

These clusters draw loosely on popular ideas about many kinds of intelligence — a useful lens for reflection, though contested as truly separate "intelligences," which is exactly why nothing here is framed as measured ability.

Walking through it

1

Answer honestly, cluster by cluster

The statements are grouped by facet and cluster, with the scale key always visible at the top. Tap the button that best fits how you actually think and act. The progress bar tracks all seventy-four.

2

Complete the calibration questions

The last two — set apart in amber — don't feed your facet scores. They gauge how confident you are in reading your own mind, which powers the self-awareness reading.

3

Read the radar and the clusters

Your profile appears as an eighteen-point radar, then as five cluster bars you can expand to see every facet. Look at the overall shape first — where it reaches out, where it draws in.

4

Sit with the four readings

Where you see yourself strongest, where you're least sure, the shape of your self-image (spiky and specialised, or even and all-round), and — the important one — how well you seem to know your own mind.

The self-awareness reading, explained

People are famously bad at rating themselves. Beginners often overestimate; genuine experts often underestimate. Psychologists call the first half of this the Dunning–Kruger effect. Mind Mirror can't tell you your true ability — but it can notice the relationship between how confident you are in your self-judgement and how much humility you report. When high confidence meets low humility, it gently flags it; when confidence and humility sit together, it says so. The most self-aware people tend to hold even their confident self-assessments loosely.

The words on the screen, in plain terms

Facet

One of the eighteen kinds of thinking you rate yourself on, each from four statements.

Cluster

A group of related facets — five in total — that make the overall picture easier to read.

The shape of your self-image

How spread out your ratings are. A spiky shape means you see yourself as specialised; an even one, as an all-rounder.

Self-awareness reading

How confidently you judge your own mind, weighed against your humility — a check on how well you know yourself, not a score of ability.

A good way to use it

  1. Answer it quickly and honestly once. Don't overthink any single statement — first instinct is usually the truest.
  2. Look at the shape before the numbers. Spiky or even? That alone says something about how you see yourself.
  3. Read the self-awareness line twice. It's the most useful sentence in the whole tool.
  4. Ask someone who knows you well to guess your radar — the gap between their view and yours is where the real insight is.
  5. Retake it in six months. Watch what's shifted. The mirror is most revealing over time.
